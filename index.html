Core Economic Principles in the U.S. Healthcare Market

A foundational analysis of the unique economic characteristics of the healthcare market, focusing on the core market failures—including moral hazard, adverse selection, and externalities—that differentiate it from standard markets.

Health Economics Market Failures Moral Hazard Adverse Selection

This analysis examines the fundamental economic principles that govern the U.S. healthcare market. It deconstructs why standard supply-and-demand models are insufficient, focusing on several key market imperfections. These include: Externalities, where individual actions like vaccination have public benefits; Asymmetric Information, where providers know more than patients; Moral Hazard, where insurance coverage may lead to overuse of services; and Adverse Selection, where sicker individuals are more likely to seek insurance, driving up premiums for everyone. Understanding these core concepts is critical for analyzing healthcare policy, system performance, and the rationale behind government and private insurer interventions.

The Triple Aim: A Framework for Healthcare Transformation

An analysis of the Institute for Healthcare Improvement's (IHI) "Triple Aim" framework, examining its theoretical basis and the practical lessons from its first seven years of implementation.

Healthcare Analytics Population Health System Design Value-Based Care

This analysis deconstructs the Triple Aim framework, a strategic model for optimizing health system performance by simultaneously pursuing three goals: improving the patient experience of care, improving the health of populations, and reducing the per capita cost of care. The initial paper by Berwick, Nolan, and Whittington outlines the theory, addressing the fragmented nature of the U.S. system and the misaligned incentives that hinder value. The follow-up paper, "Pursuing the Triple Aim: The First 7 Years," provides an applied analysis of how 141 organizations attempted to implement this framework. It identifies key success factors, such as defining a target population, establishing a robust governance structure, and creating a portfolio of projects to manage services at scale.
